"Molecular dynamics force fields:
A force field like AMBER, CHARMM, or OPLS is built differently. It's a parametrized potential energy
function — bonds, angles, dihedrals, Lennard-Jones, Coulomb — where the parameters are fit to
reproduce quantum chemistry calculations on small molecules and experimental thermodynamic data
(densities, heats of vaporization, hydration free energies). Every atom is explicit, water is
usually explicit too (TIP3P, SPC/E, etc.), and the function returns energies in real kcal/mol. The
function is differentiable in Cartesian coordinates, so you integrate Newton's equations forward in
time with femtosecond time steps to generate a trajectory. The Boltzmann distribution at the
simulation temperature is sampled (in principle, given enough time), so you can compute
thermodynamic averages, kinetics, and free energy differences with proper statistical mechanics."

\section{Proteins and their functions}

\subsection{How proteins are made}

Within cells, information of how proteins are built is encoded directly into the DNA.
At a high-level, the protein creation process can be seen as a sequence-to-sequence translation mechanism, where a 
specific sub-sequence of the DNA - composed of 4 possible \textit{nucleotide} molecules serving as its alphabet -
gets translated deterministically (except for errors) into a protein's sequence, composed of 20 possible \textit{amino-acid}
molecules.

More specifically, the process follows what is known as the central dogma of biology.
The above-mentioned sub-sequences of DNA - referred to as "coding regions" - get first \textit{transcribed}
into sequences of RNA - composed of 4 nucleotides just like DNA, with the exception of Tyrosines (T) being
converted into Uraciles (U) - then each RNA segment gets \textit{translated} into a \textit{chain} (or sequence) of 
covalently-bonded \textit{residues}, where each residue is an instance of of the 20 amino-acid types.

\subsection{Amino-acids}

\todo{(show one of the classical images of amino-acids and refer to it in the text)}

Amino-acids are the building blocks of proteins, and the diversity of protein function stems ultimately from
the diversity in the amino-acids. There are 20 possible canonical amino-acids~\footnote{there are also other
so-called non-canonical amino-acids, but they are rare and mostly get created after the RNA-protein translation
event. For simplicity, we do not considee them in this work}, with size spanning between the 5 heavy atoms
of Glycine (G) to the 14 of Tryptophan (W). All amino-acids share a substructure called the \textit{backbone},
composed of a central carbon - the C$\alpha$ - bonded to an amino group (-NH$_2$) and a carboxylic group (-COOH).
Along a protein chain, subsequent amino-acids are linked together along their backbones via \textit{peptide bonds},
whereby the carboxyl group of one reacts with the amino group of the other. What distinguishes each amino-acid is
then the variable substructure, called the \textit{side-chain} as it "dangles" on the side of the protein chain, connected to the bakbone via the C$\alpha$.
Side-chains span a wide range of chemistries, but are generally categorized in: hydrophobic, polar, positively charged,
negatively charged, and special cases, like prolines (P), which can only adopt certain backbone conformations,
cysteines (C) that can form the very stable disulfide bonds, and glycines (G) which have no side-chain.
The high diversity of chemistries in the amino-acids, coupled with the extremely high number of possible sequences
that can be created with them~\footnote{for a typical length of 400 residues, that is $20^{400}$ possible protein sequences,
which dwarves the estimated number of atoms in the universe - $10^{80}$}, generates enourmous complexity that is at the
core of proteins' functional diversity.


\subsection{The Sequence-Structure-Function paradigm}

At a very high level, the way in which a protein's amino-acid sequence results in its function is often understood
via the "sequence-structure-function" paradigm. In this description, function is understood via \textit{structure}:
the protein's sequence, owing to complex non-covalent interactions between the side-chains of the residues that compose it,
"folds" into a complex 3D structure, and it is then owing to this specific structure that the protein can carry its function.

How is this functional diversity achieved?
Just as DNA is fundamentally a long chain (also referred to as "sequence") of special molecules called nucleotides - of which there are 
4 types - each protein is fundamentally composed of a chain or sequence of small molecules called \texit{amino-acids},
of which there are 20 canonical types (plus other non-canonical types that are not considered in this thesis).
The sequential nature of both DNA and proteins is not accidental: each protein's amino-acid sequence is indeed "translated"
indirectly from a sub-sequence of DNA, through an intermediate sequence called an RNA, which is a transcription
(one-to-one mapping) of the relevant sub-sequence of DNA.

Then, the popular view is that protein function can be understood according to the sequence-structure-function paradigm.
At a high level, the paradigm states that a protein's amino-acid sequence, due to the physical forces arising from the biochemically
diverse amino-acids composing it, "folds" into a complex 3D structure, and it is then owing to this specific structure that
the protein carries its specific function. For example, to serve the purpose their very name suggests, ion channels must be
shaped with a "channel" in the center that can be opened and closed depending on specific external conditions.
