\section{Proteins and their functions}


\subsection{How proteins are made}

Within cells, information of how proteins are built is encoded directly into the DNA.
At a high-level, the protein creation process can be seen as a sequence-to-sequence translation mechanism, where a 
specific sub-sequence of the DNA - composed of 4 possible \textit{nucleotide} molecules serving as its alphabet -
gets translated deterministically (except for errors) into a protein's sequence, composed of 20 possible \textit{amino-acid}
molecules.

More specifically, the process follows what is known as the central dogma of biology.
The above-mentioned sub-sequences of DNA - referred to as "coding regions" - get first \textit{transcribed}
into sequences of RNA - composed of 4 nucleotides just like DNA, with the exception of Tyrosines (T) being
converted into Uraciles (U) - then each RNA segment gets \textit{translated} into a \textit{chain} (or sequence) of 
covalently-bonded \textit{residues}, where each residue is an instance of of the 20 amino-acid types.

\subsection{Amino-acids}

\todo{(show one of the classical images of amino-acids and refer to it in the text)}

Amino-acids are the building blocks of proteins, and the diversity of protein function stems ultimately from
the diversity in the amino-acids. There are 20 possible canonical amino-acids~\footnote{there are also other
so-called non-canonical amino-acids, but they are rare and mostly get created after the RNA-protein translation
event. For simplicity, we do not considee them in this work}, with size spanning between the 5 heavy atoms
of Glycine (G) to the 14 of Tryptophan (W). All amino-acids share a substructure called the \textit{backbone},
composed of a central carbon - the C$\alpha$ - bonded to a carboxylic group (-COOH) and an amino group (-NH$_2$) (with the exception of proline).
Along a protein chain, subsequent amino-acids are linked together along their backbones via \textit{peptide bonds},
whereby the carboxyl group of one reacts with the amino group of the other; the sub-section of a protein denoted by all
of the bonded amino-acid backbones is commonly referred to as the protein's backbone.
What distinguishes each amino-acid is then its variable substructure.
This is called the \textit{side-chain} as it "dangles" on the side of the protein chain, and it is connected to the backbone via the C$\alpha$.
Side-chains span a wide range of chemistries, but are generally categorized as follows:
hydrophobic, polar uncharged, positively charged, negatively charged, and special cases, like prolines (P), which can only adopt certain 
backbone conformations, cysteines (C) that can form the very stable disulfide bonds, and glycines (G) which have no side-chain.
The high diversity of chemistries in the amino-acids, coupled with the extremely high number of possible sequences
that can be created with them~\footnote{for a typical length of 400 residues, that is $20^{400}$ possible protein sequences,
which dwarves the estimated number of atoms in the universe - $10^{80}$}, generates enourmous complexity that is at the
core of proteins' functional diversity.


\subsection{The Sequence-Structure-Function paradigm}

At a very high level, the way in which a protein's amino-acid sequence results in its function is often understood
via the "sequence-structure-function" paradigm. In this description, the key bridge between the two is \textit{structure}:
the protein's sequence, owing to non-covalent interactions between the diverse side-chains of the residues that compose it,
as well as between residues and the immediately surrounding environment (mostly water), "folds" into a complex 3D structure,
and it is then due to the specific structure that the protein carries a specific function. A canonical example is ion channels:
to serve the purpose their name suggests, they are shaped with a "channel" in the center which a size and shape such that
they can let specific ions through at the right time.

Over the course of my PhD, I have learned that this description is useful, but its details as well as its exceptions must
be noted with care. In what follows, I will attempt to unpack this paradigm carefully.


\subsubsection{sequence $\to$ structure}

We start by noting that proteins do not generally adopt a static 3D structure: the truth is, as always, more nuanced.
As mentioned above, the protein's amino-acid composition (i.e. its sequence) determines the interactions
the protein has with itself and with the watery environment it is in.
Sometimes, the amino-acid composition is such that the protein never adopts a stable conformation: these proteins
are referred to as "intrinsically disordered", and make up around $~30\%$ of the human proteome \todo{(cite)}.
Most times, however, these interactions cause the protein to "fold" onto itself into a mostly-stable 3D structure.

There are several types of interactions and effects that contribute to a protein's fold: these include electronic interactions, 
which occur between charged side-chains, disulfide bonds, which are covalent bonds binding the Sulfur atoms of two nearby
cysteine residues, and perhaps the two most importat ones, hydrogen bonds and hydrophobic effects.
An hydrogen bond is a mostly electrostatic interaction, forming when a hydrogen atom covalently bound to an electronegative
atom (called the "donor"), interacts with another electronegative atom (the "acceptor") that has a lone pair of electrons.
Hydrogen bonds accur frequently in proteins, and are the primary drivers for the formation of \textit{secondary} structure
elements. Secondary structure is a local, regular folding pattern of the protein - in between the sequence (aka primary structure)
and the overall 3D fold (tertiary structure). There are three main secondary structure elements: $\alpha$-helices, a right-handed 
helix held together by hydrogen bonds; $\beta$-sheets, linear stretches of protein held together side-by-side via hydrogen bonds;
and finally loops, which do not have a regular shape - but can be both flexible and well-ordered - and serve as connecting regions
between the other elements.
Finally, the effect that is often cited as the primary driver of a protein's 3D fold is the hydrophobic effect.
This is the name for the tendency of hydrophobic residues - such as alanines, valines, and leucines - to "hide" from water
molecules which, since they are polar, tend to engage with polar counterparts: other water molecules and polar residues.
This effect gives rise to a particular 3D fold by forcing hydrophobic residues to be close to each other, "burying" themselves
into the core of a 3D structure that they themselves contribute to create.

So far I have talked of protein chains as if they're flexible "strands".
This flexibility does however have its limits, which are worth mentioning.
The protein chain can "bend" along two dihedral angles that from along the backbone: $\phi$ and $\psi$. 
Crucially, the range of values that these angles can take varies among the amino-acids.
All amino-acids have roughly the same admissible range, except for two: glycines and prolines.
Glycines are the most flexible: the lack of a side-chain allows for certain conformations that in other amino-acids are barred
by steric clashes.
Prolines instead are the opposite: their unique backbone geometry - with the amino group substituted by the pyrrolidine ring -
restricts the possible range of dihedral angles, making Prolines notably unsuitable for both $\alpha$-helices and $\beta$-strands.
