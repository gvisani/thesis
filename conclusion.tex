Creating accurate models of protein function, to be used for tasks in both prediction and design, is a hallmark of protein science.
Since almost all protein functions derive from the inter-atomic interations between the protein's constituent amino-acids,
both among themselves (folding) and with other biomolecules (e.g. ther proteins or small molecules),
first-principles methods rooted in physics have appeared to be the right approach to build models
that generalize across protein functions.

However, in recent years physics-based methods have fallen out of favor due to their computational complexity,
which hinders their usability.
Machine learning substitutes first-principles, general modeling with effective theories learned from data,
specific to a task and particular data modalities.
Models learned from data dispense of details that are not important for the task, resulting in effective theories
that ...


The challenge with machine learning is to develop a method that is data-efficient, and has a high chance of generalizing
to new data that was not used to fit it.

First-principles can still guide the design of the machine learning models.
For example, as a protein's binding ability is largely driven by its structure, and in particular in the composition of its binding site,
then it follows that predicting a protein's binding affinity to a target should be easier - i.e. require less data - if provided with structural information.

Prediction vs design

    Two sides of the same coin
    BUT: prediction tends to have a higher bar: there is the desire to be able to predict every edge case
    design, on the other hand, has a somewhat lower bar: often times one only cares if they can design even just a single
    protein that works, as that is all it takes

    But really, the same model can be used for prediction and design.
    A given model can be good at prediction *in a particual setting*, and therefore it will tend to design proteins
    that work *according to the setting that it is good at predicting*

    We should acknowledge this


Seeing machine learning models as hypothesis generation machines: being able to predict in all settings indicates a broadly-usable tool,
and a model that encompasses all cases in a global model. However, with machine learning we are likely never operating in such a global sense.

Instead, we are learning effective theories from data, which operate in a given regime/setting.
If a model with certain limitations works well for a prediction, it suggests that the prediction only requires the specific regime in which the model works.

The regime is defined by the assumptions (for example physical locality), but also by whatever "shortcuts" the model has learned on the data!
Assumptions are proper of hand-crafter models as well, but the other "shortcuts" are learned from the training data, and are often hard to tease out.
