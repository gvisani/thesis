
Creating accurate models describing how a protein carries its function, holds promise to both enable and speed-up
scientific discovery and translational research.
Since almost all protein functions derive from the inter-atomic interations between the protein's constituent amino-acids,
both among themselves (folding) and with other biomolecules (e.g. ther proteins or small molecules),
first-principles methods rooted in physics are established approaches for building models that generalize across
diverse protein functions.
Despite their promise of generalizability, physics based methods are still greatly limited in usability
by slow speeds and high need for computational resources.

In recent years, machine learning (ML) - in particular driven by the highly expressive neural networks - has risen to fill
this gap by substituting first-principles, general modeling
with effective theories learned from data, each specific to a single or a circumscribed set ot tasks and to a particular
representation choice.
Indeed, models learned from data dispense of details that are not important to explain the training data and task,
or that are trivially not described by the chosen representation; in short, ML models learn "shortcuts" from data.
This results in models that make predictions quickly, but whose level of accuracy in modeling the real world
depends heavily on the "shortcuts" that they have learned.
Thus, the challenge in developing an accurate and useful machine learning model rests in carefully balancing
model expressivity, training data, and choice of representation, such that the learned "shortcuts" are predictive
of real-world phenomena, much like effective theories are used in physics to model physical events
without bothering with unimportant details.

First-principles thinking can help achieve this balance.

Indeed, in protein science, the principle that a protein successfully carries its function owing to a conscribed set of
local inter-atomic effects, can help machine learning research such as much as molecular dynamics.

In this thesis, I applied this principle, and developed data-driven models of \textit{local atomic environments},
reasoning that the resulting effective theories would capture the very local inter-atomic effects that we know drive
protein function, thus rendering the models predictive across diverse functional settings.

Just like a fundamental principle underlying protein function is inter-atomic interactions, one of the core elements
of said interactions is their symmetry with respect to rotations and translations in 3D: the global orientation of the
protein or protein-protein complex has no bearing on the occurring interactions.
The field of Geometric Deep Learning offers a principled framework to constrain neural networks to make predictions
that respect desired symmetries without the use of additional training data.
This ensures that the effective theories learned by the models possess these symmetries, deleting at the root the risk
that they have learned symmetry-breaking shortcuts.

In this thesis, I have combined these first-principle considerations into a single neural network framework
for building \textit{rotationally-equivariant models of residue-centered neighborhoods}, where a neighborhood is
defined as the local atomic environment surrounding the focal residue.

In Chapter 2, I thoroughly introduced the framework at the center of this thesis.
I defined the concepts of invariance and equivariance to a specific symmetry group, identifying in equivariance the 
property we want neural networks to have.
I provided the mathematical background on equivariance - specifically to the group of rotations in 3D (known as SO(3)) -
necessary to guide the development of neural networks that respect it.
I then described the procedure for encoding a neighborhood - viewed as a spherical and featurized point could of atoms -
into a \textit{hologram}, which is then amenable to the specific SO(3)-equivariant operations that make up the
neural networks presented in this work.

In Chapter 3, I applied the SO(3)-equivariant framework to the domain of unsupervised learning, developing the Holographic (Variational) AutoEncoder (H-(V)AE).
H-(V)AE's key idea is to disentangle the latent space into a maximally informative invariant embedding - which learns a compact representation
of the input in a "canonical" orientation - and a single rotation matrix that learns the orientation of the input relative to the "canonical" one.
Through simple experiments on spherical images, we provide evidence that the "canonical" orientation is one where all the training samples are
maximally overlapped on top of each other.
I also used H-(V)AE to learn compact rotationally-invariant embeddings of residue-centered protein neighborhoods, and show that they efficiently encode key biophysical and structural
properties of the neighborhoods, and can be used as features for downstream tasks; indeed, pairing them with a Random Forest Regressor \todo{cite}
results in state-of-the-art performance on predicting protein-ligand binding affinity on the PDBBind dataset \todo{cite}.
Furthermore, H-(V)AE neighborhoods embeddings are currently being used by a colleague to easily identify structural features that are indicative
of a protein's functional family, following similar principles as~\citep{sael_improved_2010, daberdaku_exploring_2018}.

In Chapter 4, I presented a method to tackle the task known as "rotamer packing", which concerns finding physically correct side-chain
conformations that are consistent with a backbone structure and an amino-acid sequence that is predicted to fold into the backbone.
This task is common in protein design pipelines, as it complements models that design backbone structures~\citep{watson_novo_2023, lewis_scalable_2025}
and amino-acid sequences~\citep{dauparas_robust_2022} without modeling exact atomic placement of the side-chains.
The method I proposed first predicts each side-chain conformation using a local model of backbone-atom and spatially localized amino-acid labels,
and then iteratively refines the predictions using a model of the previously-predicted all-atom conformations.
The resulting algorithm - H-Packer - is not state-of-the-art on common benchmarks, but nonetheless its performance, speed and ease-of-use
have enabled it to be widely used by the community, especially through the poular BioEmu repository~\citep{}.

In Chapter 5, I revisited the work by my former colleague and mentor, Michael Pun, on H-CNN, a model trained to predict the likelihood
of finding each amino-acid in a local atomic context. In his work, Mike provided proof-of-principle results that the learned likelihoods
could be used to zero-shot the effects of mutations of a protein's stability and on protein-protein binding affinity.
In this Chapter, I contributed  several methodological improvements, culminating in the HERMES family of models.
These improvements include: a $\times 2.75$ faster and easier to optimize neural network architecture;
improved modeling of local relaxation as a result of mutations at no inference-time cost, with the model known as HERMES-\textit{amortized};
an efficient pipeline for fine-tuning HERMES on desired mutation effects, used to create HERMES-\textit{amortized}
as well as state-of-the-art models of stability effects when fine-tuning on experimental stability measurements;
a clean code repository, easy to install and use even for practitioners.
I also probed HERMES and competing models for their strengths, weaknesses,
and biases, tying them to methodological design choices. These analyses, other than serving as blueprints for the community, 
revealed that HERMES' full-atom input endows it with a strong preference for steric-preserving substitutions,
and also that pre-training on wild-type amino-acid classification introduces a wild-type preference bias that is stronger for proteins
with closer homologs in the pre-training data.
Finally, as  provided evidence that HERMES-\textit{amortized} can identify mutations known to stabilize viral antigens in a conformation
amenable for vaccine development, and presented an engineering pipeline that leveraled HERMES-\textit{amortized} to speed-up
vaccine development.

Finally, in Chapter 6, I showed further evidence for the impact that HERMES can have on biological discovery.
Specifically, I studied the interaction between a given T-Cell Receptor (TCR) and a peptide antigen presented
on an MHC molecule. These interactions are key events that regulate adaptive immunity, and their modeling has been largely elusive,
owing to a lack of exhaustive data, coupled with the high level of degeneracy characteristic of the system (a single TCR can interact
with many peptides, and vice-versa).
In this chapter, I used HERMES to model the distribution of peptides that are presented by a specific MHC, and recognized by a specific TCR,
a problem known among immunologists as that of TCR specificity.
Our results show that HERMES, without almost any specific knowledge (barring what was in the pre-training data, which deduplication at 30\% similarity greatly reduced)
of TCR-pMHC interactions, but access to a reliable wild-type TCR-pMHC structure, can be used to design a broad range of peptides that a target TCR
interacts with. HERMES not only successfully identifies peptide residues that do not contribute to TCR binding and thus can be ignored,
but is also able to suggest peptides that engage in stronger interactions with the TCR than the wild-type.
This chapter is not only a testament to HERMES' ability of modeling amino-acid substitutions in diverse structural contexts,
it is also a testament to the importance of paradigm shifts.
Indeed, prior to this work, most research on TCR specificity prediction and design was performed using sequence representations
and relied on experimental training-data distributions heavily skewed towards high TCR diversity and low peptide diversity.
Instead, using a structural representation relieves us from relying on experimental data for training, and instead enabled
the use of a zero-shot model like HERMES. This of course comes at the expense of having a reliable TCR-pMHC structure in the first place,
which is not always available and ignores any possible alternative conformation.

Overall, the documented successes of the models presented in this thesis are indicative of the effectiveness
of the design choices that underlie them.
This effectiveness is of course not absolute, as also documented in these chapters.
Therefore, in what follows, I would like to offer more perspective on the tradeoffs inherent in the design choices presented in this thesis,
and then compile them into a set of ``lessons" I have learned throughout my PhD, and that will hopefully guide me onto my next chapter.

Starting from equivariance. While enforcing equivariance certainly improves the data efficiency of neural network by reducing its function class in a manner that preserves
key symmetries, strictly equivariant models have actually fallen out of favor in recent years~\todo{cite af3 and the bitter pill molecular conformer paper}.
The reason for this has been that many equivariant architectures are generally harder to optimize: they take more epochs and have more unstable
training dynamics than more ``traditional" unconstrained architectures \todo{cite}.
While this problem seems to be relevant on a case-by-case basis, it does indicate a tradeoff between the size of the function class represented by
the neural network, and the ability of current optimization algorithms to effectively search in this function class space.
Recent works attempt to navigate this tradeoff by relaxing equivariance enough to make optimization easier.
I do not think this issue plagues the neural networks presented in this thesis, in part because, in the space of equivariant neural networks based on spherical harmonics,
they are very simple. Indeed, many alternative models store steerable tensor representations for all atoms in the input point cloud,
computing many tensor products between them~\todo{cite tensor field networks, nequip, equiformer}.
Instead, in our framework, we only hold a single, large steerable tensor representing the full point cloud.
This approach greatly coarse-grains the atomic point cloud, especially at the edges of the sphere, but such coarse graining
makes the models much faster and the optimization simpler, while being sufficiently expressive for the tasks presented in this thesis.
Furthermore, the equivariant framework shared by this thesis and by~\todo{cite tensor field networks, nequip, equiformer},
is arguably quite complex to learn and reason about if you do not have formal training in physics or group theory: I spent the better part of
the first year of my PhD just wrapping my head around it. I believe that it is also for this reason that many researchers have shifted away from
it, and have accepted the higher computational and data requirements of traditional approaches.

Moving onto protein-specific design choices, we have shown in Chapter 5 that HERMES' full-atom input endows it with a strong preference for
steric-preserving substitutions, stronger than it is observed in nature and thus often deleterious for predicting mutational effects.
Fundamentally, the issue stems from the static nature of the predictions: faster predictions are made from static stanshots, but side-chains
often readjust upon mutations, a setting that naive predictions of HERMES, specifically those of HERMES-\textit{fixed} - simply cannot model.
Adapting the side-chains to a particular mutant using Rosetta ameliorate the issue: the atomic picture is not static anymore;
however, they render the model effectively as slow as using of Rosetta itself for predicting mutation effects. 
A compromise is achieved in HERMES-\textit{amortized}, which makes predictions from the static atomic environment, but implicitly
resolved potential relaxation events.
Now, we may ask, could alternative design choices have ameliorated the issue to begin with? Of course yes.
For example, ProteinMPNN~\citep{dauparas_robust_2022} is the main competitor of HERMES for mutation effect prediction, as thoroughly discussed in Chapters 5 and 6.
Interestingly, ProteinMPNN does not display similar strong biases for steric-preserving substitutions.
In fact, its predictions, also made from a static snapshot of the wild-type structure, are unbothered by size considerations.
I attribute this to the choice made by its authors to represent side-chains
not by their exact atomic placement, but by spatially localized amino-acid labels, completely ignoring lowest-energy atomic side-chain placement.
Indeed, this strategy inherently renders ProteinMPNN's predictions more ``flexible" from a structural standpoint.
This benefit of ProteinMPNN's approach is to be contrasted with stemming from modeling proteins at a coarser level of detail
which requires more examples to learn patterns consistent with the underlying physics, and thus broadly generalizable.
With sufficient data, I believe that a coarser approach like that of ProteinMPNN would actually be preferrable to the
full-atom modeling of HERMES, for the ability of making fast predictions from static snapshots with implicit side-chain flexibility.
Nevertheless, as it stands, HERMES-\textit{amortized} appears to have the upper hand in some applications,
for example structure-based vaccine design, as demonstratedin Chapter 5.

Seeing machine learning models as hypothesis generation machines: being able to predict in all settings indicates a broadly-usable tool,
and a model that encompasses all cases in a global model. However, with machine learning we are likely never operating in such a global sense.

Instead, we are learning effective theories from data, which operate in a given regime/setting.
If a model with certain limitations works well for a prediction, it suggests that the prediction only requires the specific regime in which the model works.

The regime is defined by the assumptions (for example physical locality), but also by whatever "shortcuts" the model has learned on the data!
Assumptions are proper of hand-crafter models as well, but the other "shortcuts" are learned from the training data, and are often hard to tease out.
